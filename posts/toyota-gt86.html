toyota gt86: the “not actually fast” coupe everyone still defends

✮⋆˙ toyota and subaru make a tiny chaos pact

The Toyota GT86 (also sold as the Subaru BRZ and Scion FR-S) was basically two companies sitting down and saying: “ok, what if we stop chasing power numbers and just build something fun again?” In a world full of heavy turbo hatchbacks and crossovers, they went the opposite direction: light, simple, naturally aspirated, rear-wheel drive, manual box, and a body that looks like a mini sports car doodled in the margins of a school notebook.

It’s not a supercar, not a muscle car and not really a daily commuter either. It’s a dedicated “driver’s car”: low seating position, long nose, tiny back seats for your bag or your trauma, and a tail that clearly wants to step out if you give it half a chance. The GT86 doesn’t pretend to be practical — it pretends to be fun, which is honestly more important.

When it launched, every car journalist on earth said the same thing: “it’s not that fast, but it’s fun”. Which accidentally became its whole personality online.

✮⋆˙ the fa20 engine: torque dip, revs and opinions

Under the bonnet you get the FA20: a 2.0-liter flat-four Subaru boxer engine with Toyota’s injection magic. No turbo, no hybrid, just a moderately powered NA four-cylinder that really wants you to use the upper half of the rev range.

fa20 highlights

  • 2.0-liter naturally aspirated flat-four
  • around 200 hp stock (give or take by market/year)
  • redline around 7,400 rpm
  • not much torque, especially in the infamous “torque dip” zone
  • sounds okay stock, much better with a decent exhaust

On paper, it doesn’t look impressive. And honestly, stock, it isn’t going to snap your neck. What makes it interesting is how the engine is paired with the chassis: short gearing, light weight, and the kind of throttle response you only get from a naturally aspirated engine.

The “torque dip” (that flat, slightly dead feeling in the mid-range) is legendary at this point. Everyone complains about it, and then does intake/exhaust/tune or just drives around it by dropping a gear and using more revs. It’s mildly annoying, but also part of the car’s slightly chaotic charm.

✮⋆˙ handling: this is the whole point

The real GT86 selling point isn’t power — it’s balance. The engine is mounted low and set back, the car is light by modern standards, and the suspension is tuned so you can feel what the chassis is doing rather than just being glued to the road.

Steering is quick and feels alive, the rear is happy to move, and the whole car gives you that “slow car fast” feeling people obsess over: you can push it on a normal road without instantly hitting stupid speeds. It talks to you instead of just rocketing you forward in a straight line.

Stock tyres on early cars were famously not that grippy (basically Prius eco tyres), which just made it easier to slide around at sane speeds. Some people upgraded for grip, others kept them for maximum chaos. Either way, the car always feels like it wants you to play, not just commute.

✮⋆˙ interior: cheap plastics, perfect driving position

Inside, the GT86 is very “budget sports car”. There’s hard plastic, weird textures and nothing that feels luxurious — but the parts that matter are spot on: the wheel, the pedals, the shifter, and the seat.

You sit low, legs almost straight, wheel close, and the shifter falls perfectly to hand. The gauges are simple and focused on the rev counter, because Toyota knows what you’re actually looking at when you drive this thing properly.

The back seats are a joke for adults but perfect for bags, helmets, or pretending you “technically have a four seater” when justifying the car to other people. The boot is usable if you don’t expect it to be a wagon.

✮⋆˙ why people love it (and why people love to hate it)

The love:

  • it’s one of the few modern cars that still feels analog and playful
  • cheap-ish to run, reasonable fuel economy, Toyota/Subaru parts
  • huge aftermarket: coils, wheels, aero, exhaust, supercharger kits
  • accessible for beginners but deep enough for track nerds

The hate:

  • “it’s slow” — because people compare it to turbo hot hatches
  • torque dip memes forever
  • interior feels a bit budget in places
  • needs good tyres + alignment to really shine

But that’s kind of the point. The GT86 isn’t trying to win drag races; it’s trying to teach you about weight transfer, throttle control and lines. It’s like a driving coach disguised as a cute coupe.

For a lot of people, it’s also the first “real” fun car they can justify: not insanely expensive, not impossible to insure, and still modern enough to use daily without feeling like you’re driving a relic.

✮⋆˙ the perfect base for making it “yours”

The GT86 is basically a blank canvas. Stock, it’s already fun. But the moment you start tweaking it, the car really comes alive:

  • good coilovers or springs to get the stance and body control right
  • wheels that actually fill the arches properly
  • exhaust that lets the flat-four sound less like a vacuum, more like a car
  • tune to soften the torque dip and sharpen response
  • aero bits: lips, diffusers, wings for extra “character development”

It’s one of those rare modern cars where you can choose your flavour: clean OEM+ street car, touge-style canyon toy, stance show build, or full track rat with stripped interior and lap timer. The base is good enough that almost any direction works.

So yeah, the GT86 isn’t the fastest. But it might be one of the most honest ways to learn why driving can be fun, not just fast. And that’s why people keep defending it online like it’s their child.
