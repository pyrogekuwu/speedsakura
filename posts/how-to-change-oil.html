how to change oil: beginner-friendly guide

✮⋆˙ why changing your own oil is worth it

An oil change is one of the simplest and most satisfying bits of car maintenance you can do. It keeps your engine healthy, prevents wear, and costs way less than going to a shop. And as long as you prepare properly, it’s honestly not scary at all.

Fresh oil keeps the engine cool, reduces friction, and carries away tiny metal particles. Old oil becomes thick, dirty, and unable to protect the engine — so learning how to swap it yourself is a great first step into DIY car care.

The process is always the same: warm the engine, drain the old oil, swap the filter, fill new oil. Simple, clean (mostly), and super empowering.

✮⋆˙ what you need before you start

You only need a few basic tools and parts. Nothing exotic:

  • correct oil (check manual: viscosity + amount)
  • new oil filter
  • wrench for the drain plug
  • oil filter wrench (optional but helpful)
  • jack + jack stands or ramps
  • drain pan + funnel
  • gloves + rags

Double-check recommended oil type — using the wrong viscosity is the most common beginner mistake.

✮⋆˙ step-by-step: the actual oil change

Here’s the clean, simple flow:

  • warm the engine for 2–3 minutes so the oil flows better
  • lift the car safely on stands or ramps
  • place the drain pan under the oil plug
  • remove the plug and let the old oil drain completely
  • remove the old oil filter (some oil will drip!)
  • apply a bit of new oil to the rubber gasket of the new filter
  • install the new filter hand-tight
  • reinstall the drain plug snugly (don’t over-torque)
  • pour the new oil in through the filler cap
  • check dipstick → adjust if needed

Once done, start the engine for a few seconds and check for leaks around the filter and drain plug. If everything is dry, you’re good.

✮⋆˙ common mistakes to avoid

Newbie mistakes are super normal — here’s what to watch out for:

  • forgetting to replace the old crush washer on the drain plug
  • overtightening the filter (hand-tight is enough!)
  • adding too much oil
  • using oil with wrong viscosity
  • working under the car without proper jack stands

Most “messy oil change disasters” come from rushing or skipping small steps — so take your time.

✮⋆˙ final check: is everything right?

After 5–10 minutes of driving, recheck the dipstick. The level should be perfect — not below, not over. Also peek under the car one more time for leaks.

Once everything looks solid: you officially know how to do your own oil change ♡

It’s a small maintenance task, but it builds confidence — and saves money every single year.
