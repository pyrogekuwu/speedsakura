mazda mx-5 miata (1989–1997): the tiny roadster that ruined every other car

✮⋆˙ how mazda accidentally created a personality type

The first-generation Mazda MX-5 Miata (NA, 1989–1997) is the car that made half the internet start saying “the answer is always Miata” in every car thread. It’s a tiny, lightweight, rear-wheel drive roadster with pop-up headlights, a manual gearbox and just enough power to be fun without being scary. It’s not about numbers. It’s about vibes.

Mazda’s idea was simple and a bit romantic: recreate the feeling of old British roadsters, but without the oil leaks, constant breakdowns and electrical horror. So they took the basic formula — two seats, front engine, rear drive, soft top — and re-did it the Japanese way: reliable, well-built, and actually usable every day.

The result was a car that looked cute, drove like it really cared, and didn’t require you to be rich, skilled or brave to enjoy it. For a lot of people, the NA Miata became the first time they realised driving could be about feeling, not just speed.

✮⋆˙ engines: tiny four-cylinders, maximum serotonin

Under the bonnet, the NA Miata never pretends to be a powerhouse. You get small, revvy inline-fours that are more interested in being responsive and reliable than in winning drag races.

na miata engine flavours (simplified)

  • 1.6-liter inline-four in early cars
  • 1.8-liter inline-four in later cars
  • around 110–130 hp depending on market and year
  • loves to rev, sounds happy doing it
  • simple, tough, and very DIY-friendly

These engines aren’t fast on paper, but that’s not the point. In a car this light, with gearing this short, 120-ish horsepower suddenly becomes “enough to laugh constantly if you’re on the right road”.

People turbo them, supercharge them, engine swap them, or just keep them stock and rev them sensibly. The magic is that the car feels fun at speeds that don’t require a lawyer.

✮⋆˙ handling: slow car fast perfection

The chassis is where the Miata really becomes evil in a good way. Double wishbone suspension at all four corners, low weight, low seating position, and steering that actually tells you what’s happening — it’s like Mazda built a training tool for happiness.

The MX-5 doesn’t fight you. It invites you. Turn in, feel the front tyres load up, gently add throttle, and the rear follows. If you mess up, the car usually forgives you instead of punishing you. It’s playful, not vicious.

This is why everyone says “learn to drive in a Miata”. It teaches you about weight transfer, grip, smooth inputs and lines without needing 400 horsepower or sticky tyres. It’s like driving school, but fun.

✮⋆˙ pop-up headlights and happy little face

A huge part of the NA’s charm is visual. With the headlights down, it’s a smooth little 90s blob. With the pop-ups raised, it looks like it just saw a cat and got excited.

The proportions are almost perfect roadster math: long-ish bonnet, short tail, tiny cabin in the middle, wheels close to the corners. Roof down, it looks like a cartoon drawing of “small fun car” in the best possible way.

The interior is simple but cute: round vents, clean gauges, a steering wheel that doesn’t look like a spaceship, and controls exactly where you expect them. Everything is focused on the driver, but not in a “serious race car” way — more “hey, let’s go for a drive because it’s sunny”.

✮⋆˙ why it became a cult instead of just a cheap roadster

The Miata didn’t become a legend just because it was cheap and small. It became a legend because it combined:

  • driving feel – light, communicative, forgiving
  • reliability – it’s a Mazda, not a fragile exotic
  • mod potential – coils, wheels, exhaust, swaps, cages, whatever
  • community – owners actually wave at each other
  • vibes – it just makes people happy to look at it

It’s also incredibly flexible. Stock, it’s a cute sunny-day car. With a roll bar and good tyres, it’s a track toy. Slam it on wheels and it’s a stance build. Add a turbo and it becomes a little monster. Same base, many possible personalities.

For a lot of people, the Miata is their first “proper” fun car. It’s where they learn to heel-and-toe, spin out, fix things, bleed brakes, fit coilovers and discover that “more power” isn’t always the answer.

✮⋆˙ living with one: the good and the annoying

Day to day, an NA Miata is surprisingly easy to live with… as long as you accept what it is:

  • two seats only, and tiny storage
  • soft top that will eventually leak if abused
  • wind, noise, and some rattles – it’s a 90s roadster, not a vault
  • legit good reliability if you keep up with basic maintenance
  • park anywhere, make instant friends or get instant eye-rolls

It’s not a “do everything” car. It’s a “do one thing extremely well” car: make driving feel like a hobby instead of a chore. If you need to carry furniture, this is not it. If you want to take the long way home for no reason, this is exactly it.

✮⋆˙ why the answer is (still) miata

The NA Miata is a jdm legend not because it’s rare, but because it’s right. It proves that:

  • lightweight + balance > giant horsepower
  • reliability + fun > fragile exotic drama
  • cheap car + good roads > expensive car + traffic

Even decades later, whenever someone asks “what should I buy if I want a fun car and I’m not rich?”, the Miata always shows up in the replies. Because it works. Because it’s honest. Because it’s the kind of car that changes how you think about driving.

It might be small, a bit silly, and totally impractical in some ways — but for pure driving joy, the little NA roadster still hits harder than a lot of modern stuff that’s technically “better”.
